Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  1. Explain nuclear fission in plain terms — what splits, what's released, and why it matters
  2. Describe the chain reaction and how control rods manage it
  3. Trace the energy path: fission heat → steam → turbine → electricity

What Is Fission?

You've generated electricity from steam your whole career. The turbines, the generators, the feedwater systems — you know this equipment. Nuclear is the same thermodynamic cycle. What changes is what's making the steam.

Fission explanation (workforce-friendly):

  • Uranium-235 atoms are naturally unstable. When a slow-moving neutron hits one, it splits into two smaller atoms, releases 2-3 more neutrons, and releases an enormous amount of heat — about 50 million times more heat per atom than burning coal. (Source: DOE NNSA, "Nuclear Energy Basics")
  • Those released neutrons hit more U-235 atoms → more splits → more neutrons → chain reaction.
  • The chain reaction is controlled by control rods (made of hafnium or boron) that absorb neutrons. Full insertion = reactor shuts down. Partial insertion = power control.
Key concept: Fission doesn't burn anything. There's no flame, no combustion gases. It's pure heat from atomic splitting — and that heat is what makes steam to spin your turbine. (Source: NRC, "How Nuclear Reactors Work")
Analogy: Think of it like a billiard break shot. The cue ball (neutron) hits one ball (U-235), which sends more balls in all directions. Each of those hits another ball. Now imagine doing that 10²&sup0; times per second. That's your reactor at full power.

The Two Main US Reactor Designs

Pressurized Water Reactor (PWR) — ~65% of US fleet

  • Primary loop: water under ~2,250 psi pressure circulates through reactor core. High pressure keeps it liquid even at 550°F.
  • Steam generator: primary water transfers heat to secondary loop through tube bundles. The two loops never mix.
  • Secondary loop: lower-pressure water boils to steam, drives turbine.
  • Containment building: reinforced concrete dome around reactor and primary loop.
  • Examples: South Texas Project, Comanche Peak, Vogtle

Boiling Water Reactor (BWR) — ~35% of US fleet

  • Single loop: reactor coolant boils directly in the reactor vessel, steam goes straight to turbine.
  • Simpler (no steam generators), but turbine becomes mildly radioactive.
  • Containment: different geometry than PWR, usually with torus (suppression pool) at base.
  • Examples: Nine Mile Point, Quad Cities, Susquehanna
For trades workers: The equipment you maintain differs between PWR and BWR — PWR electricians deal with steam generator instrumentation, BWR electricians deal with a radioactive turbine building. Know which plant type you're working at.

Reactor Core

Uranium fuel rods arranged in a core. Fission happens here. The core runs at ~550°F (PWR). Control rods insert between fuel assemblies to control the chain reaction. As a trades worker: you'll maintain the motors that drive control rod mechanisms (CRDMs) and the sensors that monitor core temperature and neutron flux. (Source: NRC Technical Training Center, "Reactor Concepts Manual")
Steam Generator (PWR) / Reactor Vessel (BWR)

In a PWR, the steam generator is the heat exchanger where primary water heats secondary water through thousands of small tubes. About the size of a school bus. You'll maintain feedwater pumps, steam generator blowdown systems, level instruments, and tube plugging equipment during outages.
Turbine

Steam from the secondary loop (PWR) or directly from the reactor (BWR) drives the turbine blades. Nuclear turbines are massive — 1,000-1,400 MW units. The same equipment you know: HP and LP turbine stages, turning gear motors, lube oil systems, governor controls. Pipefitters work on steam lines and extraction systems here.
Generator

Converts mechanical rotation to electricity at typically 20-25kV. Then step-up transformers raise it to 345-500kV for the grid. You'll maintain excitation systems, voltage regulators, protective relaying (87G differential, 40 loss of field, 46 negative sequence), hydrogen cooling systems, and the main generator breaker.
Cooling Tower / Condenser

Steam exhausted from the turbine must be condensed back to liquid water for the cycle to repeat. Large plants use cooling towers (the iconic hyperbolic structures) or once-through cooling from a river or ocean. Condensate pumps (electricians maintain the motors), air ejectors, and hotwell level control are all in this area.
