What Radiation Is, How to Measure It, and How to Protect Yourself

Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  1. Identify the four types of ionizing radiation and what stops each one
  2. Explain dose units (rem, mrem, Sievert) and what the numbers mean for your body
  3. Compare everyday radiation exposures to occupational limits
  4. Apply the three ALARA tools: Time, Distance, and Shielding
  5. Distinguish between contamination and radiation — a critical difference for daily work

The Four Types of Ionizing Radiation

You'll hear "radiation" a lot at a nuclear plant. Here's the part most people get wrong: not all radiation is the same. There are four types, and each one behaves differently — which means each one is stopped differently.

Type What It Is Stopped By Hazard
Alpha (α) 2 protons + 2 neutrons (helium nucleus). Heavy, slow, +2 charge. Paper, skin, a few inches of air Dangerous if inhaled/ingested (internal). Harmless externally.
Beta (β) High-speed electron. Light, fast, -1 charge. Aluminum foil, plastic, clothing Can burn skin at high doses. Internal hazard if ingested.
Gamma (γ) Electromagnetic wave. No mass, no charge. Travels at speed of light. Lead, concrete, steel (never fully stopped — only reduced) Penetrates the whole body. This is your primary external hazard at a plant.
Neutron (n) Uncharged particle from fission. Various speeds. Water, concrete, polyethylene (hydrogen-rich materials) Highly penetrating. Found near the reactor core. Can activate materials.
For trades workers: At a nuclear plant, gamma radiation is your primary concern day-to-day. Alpha and beta sources are contained inside sealed systems. Neutrons are only a concern near the reactor vessel during operation. Your dosimeter measures gamma and neutron dose. (Source: 10 CFR 20, NRC Regulatory Guide 8.34)

Understanding Dose: What the Numbers Mean

Radiation dose measures how much energy radiation deposits in your body. Here are the units you'll use every day at a plant:

  • rem (Roentgen Equivalent Man) — the US standard. 1 rem = significant dose.
  • mrem (millirem) — 1/1000 of a rem. Most occupational doses are measured in mrem.
  • Sievert (Sv) — international standard. 1 Sv = 100 rem. 1 mSv = 100 mrem.
NRC Occupational Limits (10 CFR 20.1201):
• 5,000 mrem/year (5 rem) — whole body (TEDE)
• 50,000 mrem/year (50 rem) — any individual organ
• 15,000 mrem/year (15 rem) — lens of the eye
• 50,000 mrem/year (50 rem) — skin or extremities
Most plants set administrative limits well below these — typically 1,000-2,000 mrem/year.
Context matters: The average American receives about 620 mrem/year from natural background radiation (radon, cosmic rays, food, medical procedures). A nuclear plant worker's occupational dose is typically 100-300 mrem/year — less than a single CT scan. (Source: NCRP Report No. 160)

ALARA — As Low As Reasonably Achievable

ALARA isn't just a rule — it's a way of thinking. Every dose you receive should be as low as reasonably achievable, considering economic and practical factors. You have three tools to reduce dose:

  • Time: Spend less time near the source. Plan your work, practice your task, minimize time in radiation areas. A 50% reduction in time = 50% reduction in dose.
  • Distance: The inverse square law — double your distance, quarter your dose. Even a few extra feet matter. If the source is at 1 foot and you move to 2 feet, your dose rate drops by 75%.
  • Shielding: Put material between you and the source. Lead blankets, concrete walls, water — each material has a "half-value layer" (HVL) that cuts dose in half.
Think of it like a campfire: To get less heat (dose), you can:
• Spend less time near it (Time)
• Stand further back (Distance)
• Put a wall between you and it (Shielding)
Same physics, different energy source.
Contamination vs. Radiation — know the difference:
• Radiation is energy traveling from a source. When you leave the area, you stop receiving dose. Like standing near a light bulb — walk away and the light stops hitting you.
• Contamination is radioactive material ON you or IN you. It travels with you and keeps giving you dose until it's removed. Like getting paint on your hands — it doesn't stop until you wash it off.
This is why you wear protective clothing (PCs), booties, and gloves in contaminated areas — and why you pass through a monitor when leaving. (Source: NRC NUREG-1556, Vol. 18)
