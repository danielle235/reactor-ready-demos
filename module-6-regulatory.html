  2. Identify key regulations: 10 CFR 50 (reactor licensing), 10 CFR 20 (radiation protection), 10 CFR 73 (security)

Who Watches the Watchmen? — The NRC

Nuclear power in the United States is one of the most regulated industries in the world. The agency responsible? The Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC) — an independent federal agency that exists for one purpose: to protect the public health and safety and the environment.

The NRC was created in 1975 when Congress split the old Atomic Energy Commission (AEC) in two. The AEC had a conflict of interest — it was supposed to both promote nuclear energy AND regulate it. Congress decided those two jobs needed to be separated. The NRC got regulation. The Department of Energy got promotion and research.

Here's how the NRC is structured:

Level Who / What Role
Commissioners 5 commissioners, appointed by the President, confirmed by Senate. Max 3 from any one political party. Set policy, approve regulations, issue licenses. Serve 5-year terms.
Headquarters Rockville, Maryland Rulemaking, national policy, license review, major enforcement actions.
4 Regional Offices Region I (King of Prussia, PA), II (Atlanta, GA), III (Lisle, IL), IV (Arlington, TX) Inspections, enforcement for plants in their region.
Resident Inspectors 2 per plant (minimum), stationed on-site permanently Monitor day-to-day operations, review maintenance, investigate events. They have an office INSIDE your plant.
💡 Analogy: Think of the NRC like a building inspector who doesn't just show up once when the building is complete. They have a permanent office on the construction site. They watch every weld, every concrete pour, every electrical panel. And they have the authority to stop work at any time. That's what NRC resident inspectors do — 24/7, year-round, at every US nuclear plant.
Fun fact: NRC resident inspectors can enter any part of the plant at any time, without prior notice, day or night. They report directly to the regional office — not to plant management. This independence is by design. (Source: NRC Inspection Manual, Chapter 0102)

The Rulebook — Key Regulations

NRC regulations live in Title 10 of the Code of Federal Regulations (10 CFR). Each part covers a different aspect of nuclear operations. As a trades worker, you'll encounter these most often:

Regulation What It Covers Why It Matters to You
10 CFR 50 Domestic Licensing of Production and Utilization Facilities The reactor license itself. Everything the plant is allowed to do — and not do — flows from this license. Violating your license is a federal offense.
10 CFR 20 Standards for Protection Against Radiation Your dose limits, ALARA requirements, contamination controls. The rules behind your dosimeter readings and radiation work permits.
10 CFR 73 Physical Protection of Plants and Materials Security rules — access control, vital area boundaries, the armed security force, cyber security. Why you badge in and why you NEVER hold a door open.
10 CFR 26 Fitness for Duty Programs Drug and alcohol testing, fatigue management, mandatory rest requirements. The federal rules that govern your work hours and testing schedule.
10 CFR 55 Operators' Licenses Who can control the reactor. Licensed reactor operators (RO) and senior reactor operators (SRO) must pass NRC exams. Not your license as a tradesperson, but you'll work alongside these people.

Beyond federal regulations, each plant has its own set of rules called Technical Specifications (Tech Specs). Tech Specs are derived from the plant's Safety Analysis Report and approved by the NRC as part of the operating license. They contain specific, binding limits — things like minimum water levels, maximum temperatures, and which safety systems must be operable.

🚨 Technical Specifications are NOT suggestions: Every nuclear plant has a Technical Specifications book — hundreds of pages of specific limits and conditions. If a Tech Spec is violated, it triggers a Limiting Condition for Operation (LCO). The plant must take corrective action within a defined time window or shut down. Violations must be reported to the NRC. This is the most serious day-to-day regulatory boundary at any plant. (Source: 10 CFR 50, Appendix A, General Design Criteria)
Defense in Depth: You'll hear this phrase constantly in nuclear. It means no single layer of protection is relied upon — multiple independent barriers exist. The fuel cladding, the reactor pressure vessel, the containment building — each is an independent layer. Regulations are designed to protect all of these layers simultaneously. (Source: IAEA Safety Standards Series No. SSR-2/1)

Getting Your Badge — Access Authorization

Before you can work unescorted at a nuclear plant, you go through Access Authorization — one of the most thorough background investigations outside of government security clearances. Plan for 4–8 weeks. It is not optional, and it cannot be rushed.

Here's what the investigation covers:

  • Criminal history check — federal, state, local, and international where applicable
  • Credit history — financial stress is considered a potential security risk
  • Employment verification — 5–10 years of work history confirmed
  • Education verification — degrees and certifications confirmed
  • References — character references interviewed by security
  • Psychological evaluation — not just for operators; required for many roles with unescorted access
  • Drug screening — initial test plus ongoing random testing under 10 CFR 26
For trades workers: As an electrician, pipefitter, or welder doing nuclear outage work, you will go through this process. Some plants use a PADS (Personnel Access Data System) — a shared database so background checks from one plant can transfer to another. If you've worked nuclear before and maintained good standing, re-authorization may be faster. (Source: 10 CFR 73.56)

Once cleared, you'll receive a badge granting unescorted access to specified plant areas. The Fitness for Duty program (10 CFR 26) then governs your ongoing eligibility:

10 CFR 26 Requirement What It Means for You
Random drug/alcohol testing You can be selected for testing any day, any time. No advance notice. A positive result = immediate removal of unescorted access.
Maximum work hours 16 hours max in any 24-hour period. 26 hours max in any 48-hour period. 72 hours max in any 7-day period.
Minimum rest 10 hours off between shifts (minimum). You must report fatigue to your supervisor if you cannot safely perform your job.
Self-reporting You must self-report any arrest, criminal charge, or change in fitness status — even off-site and off-hours.
💡 Think of it this way: Commercial airline pilots have strict rest requirements and random testing. Nuclear workers have similar standards — because the consequences of impaired judgment are severe in both fields. The fatigue rules exist because real accidents have been caused by tired workers making preventable mistakes.

Beyond Compliance — The Safety Culture Pyramid

Regulations are the floor, not the ceiling. The nuclear industry has built a culture of voluntary excellence on top of the regulatory minimum. Four pillars hold up this culture:

Organization / Program What It Does Your Role
INPO
Institute of Nuclear Power Operations		 Industry self-regulation body. Evaluates every US plant on a regular cycle. INPO ratings affect plant reputation, insurance, and Wall Street. You may encounter INPO evaluators during plant visits. Their findings carry real weight — sometimes more than NRC inspections.
SAT
Systematic Approach to Training		 Nuclear's training methodology. Analysis → Design → Development → Implementation → Evaluation (ADDIE-based). Every training task is tied to a job analysis. Every task you're trained to do in nuclear has been analyzed, designed, and verified. "We've always done it this way" is NOT an acceptable answer.
CAP
Corrective Action Program		 Every problem, near-miss, or concern gets documented in a Condition Report (CR). CRs are tracked, investigated, and resolved. No problem is too small to write up. You are expected to write CRs. Finding and reporting problems is GOOD. Hiding problems — or not writing them up — is a violation of safety culture.
OE
Operating Experience		 Lessons learned from events at any plant — worldwide — are shared and applied everywhere. If a plant in France has a maintenance error, US plants review it. You may receive OE briefings before jobs. Pay attention. Someone else already paid the price for that lesson.
🚨 The most important rule in nuclear safety culture: "I didn't know" is never acceptable. The industry has systems — training, procedures, pre-job briefings, operating experience, stop-work authority — specifically designed to make sure you DO know. If you don't understand something, you have both the right and the responsibility to stop work and ask.
Stop-Work Authority: Every worker at a nuclear plant — from the most experienced engineer to a first-day contractor — has the right to stop any job they believe is unsafe or doesn't match the procedure. Using stop-work authority is NOT career-limiting. Failing to use it when you should have is. (Source: NRC Safety Culture Policy Statement, 76 FR 34773)
