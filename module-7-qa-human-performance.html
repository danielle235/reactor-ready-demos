Nuclear Quality Is Different — NQA-1

In most industries, quality means doing good work that meets spec. In nuclear, quality means something more demanding: provably correct, documented, and auditable for the life of the plant. That distinction changes everything about how work gets done.

NQA-1 (ASME Nuclear Quality Assurance standard) is the governing quality framework for nuclear construction, operation, and maintenance. It defines 18 criteria that cover the entire lifecycle of safety-related work — from how your organization is structured to how inspections are conducted and how problems get corrected.

  • Organization & Management: Who is responsible for quality, how authority is assigned, independence of QA oversight
  • Design Control: Calculations must be reviewed and approved; changes must go through the same rigor as originals
  • Procurement: Parts and materials for safety-related systems require documented pedigree — not just a receipt
  • Instructions, Procedures, Drawings: Written, approved documents guide every safety-related task — no improvisation
  • Inspection: Independent verification of completed work by a qualified QC inspector, separate from the craft worker
  • Nonconforming Items: Anything that doesn't meet spec gets tagged, documented, and dispositioned — never quietly set aside
  • Corrective Action: Every identified problem gets documented, analyzed, and fixed — then tracked to closure
Commercial vs. Nuclear — Same Skill, Different Documentation:
A welder in commercial construction proves they did good work by the weld holding. A welder in nuclear proves good work with: a written procedure, a qualified process, a qualified welder certificate, pre-heat records, inspection hold points, NDE (non-destructive examination) results, a QC inspector sign-off, and a traveler document that will be stored for 40+ years. The weld is the same. The evidence trail is completely different.
For trades workers: Every weld, every cable pull, every valve replacement in a nuclear plant has a Quality Control inspector watching and a paper trail that will be auditable for the life of the plant. You don't just sign your name to say it's done — you sign your name to say it was done correctly, to the approved procedure, with the right parts, under the right conditions. (Source: ASME NQA-1; 10 CFR 50 Appendix B)

Human Performance Tools — Your Daily Toolkit

Human error is the leading contributor to nuclear events. The industry's response wasn't to blame workers — it was to develop systematic tools that make errors less likely and easier to catch before they matter. These aren't bureaucratic checkboxes. They're practical habits that every nuclear worker uses every day, on every safety-related task.

STAR
Stop, Think, Act, Review. Before every task, pause. What am I about to do? What could go wrong? Did I do it right? Use it before every safety-related action — especially the ones you've done a hundred times before.
Self-Checking
Touch, verify, then act. Point at the component. Read the label aloud. Confirm it matches the procedure. Then perform the action. One step at a time — not two steps ahead.
Peer Checking
Two sets of eyes on critical steps. For safety-related work, a second qualified person independently verifies the action (concurrent verification) or the result (independent verification). The checker doesn't just watch — they verify independently.
Pre-Job Brief
Every job starts with a team meeting. Scope of work, hazards (radiation, electrical, fall), special instructions, contingency plan if something unexpected happens. No job starts until every person on the crew understands the plan.
3-Way Communication
Sender states → Receiver repeats back → Sender confirms. Prevents miscommunication for verbal instructions. If you can't confirm a readback, don't proceed. Used constantly in control rooms and during critical maintenance evolutions.
Phonetic Alphabet
Alpha, Bravo, Charlie, Delta... Prevents misidentification of components over radio. "B" and "D" sound alike. "Bravo" and "Delta" don't. Used when verbally identifying components — especially valves, breakers, and instrument tags.
Procedure Use & Adherence
Steps are read, performed, and signed off one at a time. You don't read ahead and work from memory. You don't skip steps because they seem obvious. You don't fill in the blanks after the fact. The procedure is your authorization to work.
Place-Keeping
Track exactly where you are in a procedure. Use a physical marker (finger, card) on the current step. If interrupted, mark your place, note your status, don't proceed from memory. Interruption is one of the biggest error precursors in nuclear work.
For trades workers: You already know how to pull wire and terminate panels. In nuclear, you'll also learn to STAR before every termination, peer-check every safety-related connection, and use the phonetic alphabet when identifying terminals on a radio call. The skill doesn't change. The discipline does. (Source: INPO Human Performance Reference Manual; INPO 06-003)

When Things Go Wrong — The Corrective Action Program

Every nuclear plant has a Corrective Action Program (CAP). It is the single most important quality system in the plant. The premise is simple: if you find a problem and document it, you can fix it before it matters. If you don't document it, it will compound until it does.

When you identify any issue — a leaking fitting, a procedure that seems wrong, a near-miss, an equipment anomaly, or even a "something feels off" — you write a Condition Report (CR). The CR is reviewed, triaged by severity, and assigned for resolution.

  • Condition Report (CR): The entry point. Anyone can write one. Low threshold for entry — when in doubt, write it.
  • Apparent Cause Evaluation (ACE): Used for moderate-significance issues. Identifies the most apparent cause and corrective actions.
  • Root Cause Analysis (RCA): Required for significant events. Structured investigation to find the fundamental cause — not just the symptom — and the organizational factors behind it.
  • Trending: CAP data is analyzed for patterns. Ten separate minor CRs about the same system might trigger a deeper look. The system is designed to catch patterns before they become events.
  • Operating Experience (OE): INPO distributes lessons learned from events at other plants. Your plant is expected to review applicable OE and determine if corrective actions are needed.
Think of CAP like a near-miss reporting system at a construction site — but mandatory and comprehensive. Except in nuclear, you don't just report near-misses. You report anything that isn't quite right, any procedure that's unclear, any tool that might not be calibrated. The culture is: finding a problem is good, hiding a problem is the worst thing you can do.
⚠ Career-ending move: Hiding a problem in nuclear — bypassing a deficiency, failing to tag out nonconforming equipment, not writing a CR because you "fixed it yourself" — is a career-ending decision. The culture rewards finding and reporting problems. It has zero tolerance for concealment. This isn't a legal technicality; it's how Three Mile Island and Davis-Besse happened — small problems that weren't reported, compounding quietly until they weren't small anymore. (Source: 10 CFR 50 Appendix B, Criterion XVI; INPO AP-913)

The Questioning Attitude

Nuclear's most important cultural principle isn't a tool or a procedure — it's a mindset. The questioning attitude means never assuming that the way things look is the way things are, and never proceeding when something doesn't seem right.

  • "What could go wrong?" isn't pessimism — it's professional rigor. Running through failure modes before you act is expected, not neurotic.
  • Procedure compliance: If a procedure step seems wrong, unclear, or doesn't match plant conditions — STOP. Don't deviate and don't freelance. Contact your supervisor, the procedure owner, or engineering. A procedure change request (PCR) is the path forward.
  • Technical curiosity: You're expected to understand WHY you're doing something, not just HOW. Blind compliance without understanding breeds error. If a step doesn't make sense, ask until it does.
  • Raising concerns: The NRC requires every licensee to maintain a Safety Conscious Work Environment (SCWE). You have the right — and the professional obligation — to raise safety concerns without fear of retaliation.
  • Employee protection: 10 CFR 50.7 prohibits discrimination against employees who raise safety concerns. This includes internal concerns, concerns raised to the NRC, or participation in NRC investigations. These are federal protections.
The Davis-Besse Lesson (2002): Reactor head corrosion was discovered at the Davis-Besse plant that had eaten nearly all the way through the carbon steel vessel head, leaving only a thin stainless steel liner. The corrosion had been developing for years. Multiple opportunities to identify it were missed because workers and supervisors normalized what they saw — boric acid deposits are common, nothing seemed dramatically wrong. The questioning attitude means not normalizing abnormal conditions. "It's always looked like that" is not an acceptable answer in nuclear. (Source: NRC Special Inspection Report 50-346/02-09; NRC Event Report)
For trades workers: In most industries, raising concerns makes you difficult. In nuclear, NOT raising concerns makes you a liability. The culture actively rewards people who stop work and ask questions. If you're ever in a situation where stopping to ask feels uncomfortable — that discomfort is the system working. Trust it. (Source: NRC RIS 2005-18; INPO 12-012 SCWE)
