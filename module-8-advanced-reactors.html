Why New Reactors? — The Case for Advanced Nuclear

The US fleet of ~94 operating reactors is one of the most impressive engineering achievements in history. It also has a problem: every single one of those plants was designed in the 1960s and 1970s. They're Generation II designs, and most are running decades past their original 40-year license terms.

We've learned a lot since then. Fukushima in 2011 showed what happens when active safety systems lose power. Vogtle Units 3 & 4 in Georgia showed what happens when you try to build a Gen III+ plant with the old construction model — 10 years and $35 billion for two reactors. That's not scalable.

The nuclear industry responded with two parallel tracks: Small Modular Reactors (SMRs) and advanced reactor designs. These aren't just smaller reactors. They represent a fundamental rethinking of how nuclear power is designed, built, and operated.

The LEGO analogy: Traditional large reactors are like custom-built mansions — every component designed and built on site, years of construction, enormous cost overruns. SMRs are like modular homes: standardized modules built in a controlled factory environment, shipped to the site, and assembled in a fraction of the time. Same quality. Radically different economics.
  • Current US fleet: ~94 reactors, all Generation II/III designs, average age over 40 years
  • The cost problem: Vogtle 3&4 took 10+ years and cost $35 billion — the old model doesn't scale
  • SMR promise: Factory-built modules, predictable construction timelines, lower up-front capital
  • Advanced reactor promise: New fuels, new coolants, passive safety (no operator action needed in emergencies)
  • DOE investment: $3.2 billion+ committed to advanced reactor demonstrations as of 2024
Key stat: The NRC approved the first-ever SMR design certification (NuScale) in 2022 — the first new reactor type certified in over 20 years. The regulatory pathway now exists. The industry is moving. (Source: NRC Design Certification Docket No. 52-048)

Meet the New Reactors — A Field Guide

Five designs are leading the advanced reactor buildout in North America. Here's what you need to know about each one — what's different about it, where it's being built, and what trades it needs.

BWR • 300 MWe
GE Hitachi BWRX-300
What's different: A radically simplified boiling water reactor — 10x fewer pumps, valves, and active safety systems compared to a traditional BWR. GE calls it "simplification by elimination." The core sits in a below-grade water pool; passive gravity-driven cooling kicks in automatically if anything goes wrong.

Where it's going: Ontario Power Generation is building one at the Darlington site in Canada — the first SMR in the G7. TVA is evaluating Clinch River, TN as a potential US site.

Status: Pre-licensing engagement with NRC underway; OPG construction begins mid-2020s.

Trades angle: The system design is familiar — it's still a BWR. Electricians, pipefitters, welders, I&C technicians all apply the same core skills. Fewer systems means faster outages and simpler maintenance.
Electricians Pipefitters Welders I&C Technicians Millwrights
LWR • 77 MWe/module
NuScale VOYGR
What's different: Up to 12 small reactor modules, each 77 MWe, all submerged inside a single water pool. Passive cooling by convection — no pumps, no diesel generators, no operator action required for cooling in an emergency. The entire module is factory-fabricated and shipped by truck or rail.

Status: NRC Design Certification granted 2022 — the first SMR ever certified. The original Idaho project was paused due to rising costs, but the certified design is available globally. International customers are actively evaluating.

Trades angle: Factory module assembly creates a new class of nuclear construction work — less on-site construction, more factory-floor QA. Site work focuses on module installation and integration. New paradigm for construction trades.
Module Assemblers Rigging & Crane Ops Structural Welders NDE Inspectors I&C Technicians
FHR • 35 MWt test
Kairos Power Fluoride Salt‑Cooled (Hermes)
What's different: Completely different from water-cooled reactors. Uses molten fluoride salt as the coolant and TRISO pebble fuel — tiny ceramic spheres the size of billiard balls, each containing thousands of microscopic fuel particles with built-in containment layers. The salt stays liquid at atmospheric pressure (no pressurization needed).

Where it's going: Building the Hermes test reactor in Oak Ridge, TN. NRC issued construction permit in 2023 — the first advanced non-light-water reactor permitted in the US in 50 years.

Trades angle: This is new territory. Molten salt requires specialized piping (high-temp, corrosion-resistant alloys), new instrumentation, and training in salt chemistry. Trades workers who cross-train on these systems early will be in high demand.
High-Temp Pipefitters Alloy Welders I&C Technicians Chemical Operators Insulation Workers
HTGR • 80 MWe/module
X-energy Xe-100
What's different: A high-temperature gas-cooled reactor using helium as the coolant and TRISO fuel. Operates at much higher temperatures than water-cooled reactors — which means it can produce both electricity AND industrial process heat. This unlocks entirely new markets: chemical plants, hydrogen production, steel manufacturing.

Where it's going: Dow Chemical is partnering to deploy at a Texas industrial site — the first advanced reactor designed specifically for industrial energy users, not just the grid.

Trades angle: Helium systems are closer to HVAC and gas systems than traditional nuclear. Gas system specialists, HVAC technicians, and instrument techs with gas experience are in demand. Industrial site deployment means new geographic markets.
Gas System Techs HVAC Specialists I&C Technicians High-Temp Welders Turbine Mechanics
SFR • 345 MWe
TerraPower Natrium
What's different: A sodium-cooled fast reactor with an integrated molten salt energy storage system. Sodium coolant allows the reactor to run hotter than water and avoids pressurization. The energy storage "tank" can absorb excess power during low-demand periods and release it during peak demand — making nuclear power dispatchable like a peaker plant.

Where it's going: Building in Kemmerer, Wyoming — specifically at a retiring coal plant site. Backed by Bill Gates and designed to provide jobs for coal communities transitioning to clean energy.

Trades angle: Sodium handling is specialized — it burns on contact with water and air, requiring sealed systems and unique safety protocols. Workers trained in sodium systems will command premium wages. The Wyoming project is actively hiring across all construction trades.
Sodium System Techs Pipefitters (specialty) I&C Technicians Electricians Civil & Structural

Passive Safety — The Game Changer

The biggest difference between the current fleet and advanced reactors isn't size or fuel — it's how they stay safe when things go wrong. Understanding this difference is essential for anyone entering the nuclear workforce.

Traditional (active) safety: Relies on pumps, diesel generators, valves, and operator actions to maintain cooling. If power is lost, you have minutes to restore it before the fuel begins to overheat. Systems must activate on command. Operators must respond correctly and quickly.

Passive safety: Uses gravity, natural convection, stored energy, and physics — not pumps or operator action — as the primary safety mechanism. Emergency cooling water sits above the reactor and drains by gravity. Natural circulation moves coolant without pumps. Even if every operator leaves the control room, the reactor safely cools itself.

Why this matters — Fukushima: The Fukushima Daiichi accident was caused by loss of AC power followed by loss of DC backup power. Active cooling systems failed. The fuel overheated and melted. Every advanced reactor design treats passive safety as a primary safety feature, not a backup — specifically to prevent a Fukushima-type scenario. (Source: NRC SECY-11-0093)
Passive safety doesn't mean "no operators needed." It means operators have hours or days to respond instead of minutes. Advanced reactors are inherently safer AND they make the operator's job less demanding in emergencies. Both things are true. (Source: IAEA SSR-2/1)
  • BWRX-300: Below-grade pool provides weeks of passive cooling with no operator action
  • NuScale VOYGR: Reactor submerged in water; natural convection cooling requires zero power
  • Kairos FHR: TRISO fuel contains fission products even at very high temperatures; salt chemistry is inherently stable
  • Xe-100: TRISO fuel cannot melt under any operating scenario; helium is chemically inert
  • Natrium: Sodium coolant at atmospheric pressure; large thermal mass slows any transient significantly

Your Career in Advanced Nuclear — What's Different

The advanced reactor buildout is not a future prediction. It's happening right now. TVA, Ontario Power Generation, Dow Chemical, and the state of Wyoming are all actively constructing or permitting advanced nuclear projects. The bottleneck isn't money or technology — it's qualified workforce.

Core skills still matter: Welding, electrical, pipefitting, instrumentation & control — the fundamentals don't change. Every advanced reactor still needs these skills. The difference is where those skills are applied and what additional cross-training adds value.

Skills that transfer directly (no new training required):

Welding (ASME Code) Electrical (NEC) Pipefitting I&C Installation Civil & Structural HVAC Rigging & Crane Ops QA/QC Inspection

New skills in demand (cross-training opportunity with premium pay):

Modular Construction & Assembly Factory-to-Site Logistics Sodium System Operations Molten Salt Systems TRISO Fuel Handling Digital I&C (vs. analog) NDE — Ultrasonic & Radiographic High-Temp Alloy Welding Helium/Gas System Maintenance

Geographic opportunities — where the jobs are:

  • Kemmerer, Wyoming — TerraPower Natrium (former coal plant site; coal trades welcome)
  • Oak Ridge, Tennessee — Kairos Hermes test reactor (under construction now)
  • Darlington, Ontario — BWRX-300 (US trades workers eligible for cross-border projects)
  • Texas Gulf Coast — X-energy Xe-100 at Dow Chemical
  • Clinch River, Tennessee — TVA evaluating BWRX-300
Don't wait for "someday." The nuclear renaissance is not a future prediction — it's happening NOW. Kemmerer is breaking ground. Oak Ridge is under NRC construction permit. Ontario has started site prep. The workforce pipeline is the bottleneck. Trades workers who get nuclear-qualified NOW will have the most options as these projects ramp up.
