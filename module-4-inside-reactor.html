1. The Reactor Vessel & Core Components

The reactor pressure vessel (RPV) is the heart of a nuclear power plant. This massive steel vessel — typically 40-45 feet tall, 14-20 feet in diameter, with walls 8-12 inches thick — contains the nuclear fuel, control rods, and coolant that make power generation possible.

Source: Lamarsh & Baratta, "Introduction to Nuclear Engineering," Ch. 4

Core Components

Component Function Material Trades Relevance
Fuel Assemblies Contain UO₂ fuel pellets in zircaloy cladding; where fission occurs UO₂ pellets, Zircaloy-4 cladding Handled by reactor engineers during refueling
Control Rods Absorb neutrons to control reaction rate; inserted = less power Ag-In-Cd alloy or B₄C Drive mechanisms maintained by I&C and electricians
Reactor Coolant Removes heat from core; acts as neutron moderator Light water (H₂O), borated Pumps, valves, piping maintained by all trades
Core Barrel Structural support directing coolant flow through fuel assemblies Stainless steel Inspected during refueling outages
Pressurizer (PWR) Maintains RCS pressure at ~2,235 psi to prevent boiling Carbon steel, SS-clad Heaters & spray valves maintained by electricians & mechanics

Source: USNRC, "Reactor Concepts Manual," NUREG/BR-0298, Rev. 4

Key Concept: Everything in the reactor vessel serves one of three purposes: (1) sustain the chain reaction, (2) remove heat, or (3) control the reaction rate. Understanding this simplifies the entire system.

2. Safety Systems & Defense in Depth

Nuclear power plants are designed with multiple independent layers of protection — a philosophy called Defense in Depth. If one barrier fails, the next one is ready. No single failure can lead to a release of radioactivity.

Source: IAEA, "Defence in Depth in Nuclear Safety," INSAG-10

The Five Physical Barriers

  1. Fuel Pellet: Ceramic UO₂ retains ~98% of fission products within its crystal structure
  2. Fuel Cladding: Zircaloy tube surrounds each fuel pellet — first engineered barrier
  3. Reactor Vessel: 8-12 inch thick carbon steel with stainless steel cladding
  4. Primary Containment: Steel-lined, reinforced concrete structure (3-4 ft thick walls)
  5. Secondary Containment / Shield Building: Outer concrete structure (in some designs)

Source: USNRC, "Backgrounder on Defense-in-Depth," 2021

Engineered Safety Features

System Acronym Purpose Activation
Emergency Core Cooling ECCS Flood core with borated water after LOCA Automatic on low RCS pressure
Containment Spray CSS Reduce containment pressure & scrub radioactive iodine Automatic on high containment pressure
Emergency Diesel Generators EDG Backup power if offsite power is lost Auto-start within 10 seconds on LOOP signal
Reactor Protection System RPS Automatic reactor trip (SCRAM) on abnormal conditions Multiple redundant sensor channels
Auxiliary Feedwater AFW Backup water supply to steam generators for decay heat removal Auto or manual on loss of main feedwater

Source: USNRC, "Reactor Concepts Manual," Ch. 6; 10 CFR 50.46

Why This Matters to Trades Workers: Every safety system requires electrical power, instrumentation, and mechanical components — maintained by the electricians, I&C technicians, and mechanics who keep the plant running. When you test an EDG, you're testing the last line of defense.

3. How Operators Control the Reactor

Licensed reactor operators control the nuclear chain reaction using three primary methods. Understanding these helps trades workers appreciate why certain maintenance activities are safety-critical.

Three Methods of Reactivity Control

Method Mechanism Speed Use Case
Control Rods Mechanical insertion/withdrawal of neutron-absorbing material Fast (seconds) Load following, SCRAM (emergency shutdown)
Boron Concentration Dissolving boric acid in coolant to absorb neutrons Slow (hours) Long-term reactivity management, fuel burnup compensation
Temperature Feedback Negative temperature coefficient — hotter core = less reactivity Inherent (automatic) Self-regulating safety feature (no operator action needed)

Source: Lamarsh & Baratta, Ch. 7; USNRC Reactor Concepts Manual, Ch. 3

Negative Temperature Coefficient: In most modern reactors, if the core temperature rises, the chain reaction naturally slows down. This is a built-in safety feature — the physics itself acts as a brake. It's one reason why a Chernobyl-type accident is physically impossible in US-designed reactors (which use a negative void coefficient, unlike the RBMK's positive one).
Source: MIT OCW 22.01, Lecture 26 (Chernobyl analysis)

What Happens During a SCRAM

A reactor trip (SCRAM) is the emergency shutdown of the reactor. When triggered, all control rods drop into the core within 2-4 seconds by gravity (the motors release, not insert them — a fail-safe design). The chain reaction stops almost instantly.

But decay heat remains. Fission products continue generating about 7% of full power immediately after shutdown, declining to ~1% within an hour. This is why cooling must continue even after the reactor is "off."

Source: ANS Standard 5.1-2014 (Decay Heat Standard); USNRC NUREG-1150

Decay Heat Is Why Fukushima Happened: The reactors SCRAMmed successfully during the earthquake. But when the tsunami destroyed the backup diesel generators, there was no power to run the cooling pumps. Without cooling, decay heat melted the fuel — even though the chain reaction had already stopped.
Source: INPO 11-005, "Special Report on the Nuclear Accident at Fukushima"

4. What Trades Workers Actually Maintain

A nuclear power plant employs hundreds of skilled trades workers. Here's what each trade touches — and why every job is safety-critical.

Trade Key Systems Safety-Critical Work Examples
Electricians 4.16kV safety buses, EDGs, motor control centers, cable routing EDG maintenance, breaker testing, Class 1E circuit work
I&C Technicians RPS, process sensors, control rod drive mechanisms, PAMS Safety channel calibration, RPS logic testing
Mechanics / Pipefitters RCS pumps, valves, heat exchangers, ECCS piping Safety valve testing, RCP seal replacement
Welders ASME III piping, structural supports, containment penetrations Safety-related welds per ASME Section IX, NDE

Source: NEI 06-13, "Template for an Industry Training Program"; 10 CFR 50.120

Class 1E: This is the NRC classification for electrical equipment and systems that are essential to reactor safety. Any work on Class 1E equipment requires special procedures, qualified personnel, and documented quality assurance. As a nuclear electrician, most of your critical work involves Class 1E systems.
Source: IEEE 308-2020, "Standard Criteria for Class 1E Power Systems"
